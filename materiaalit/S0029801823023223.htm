A resolved SPH-DEM coupling method for analysing the interaction of polyhedral granular materials with fluid

Abstract

Hybrid fluid–particle systems are prevalent in nature and engineering practices, but accurately simulating the dynamic behaviour is challenging due to their inherent strong non-linearity. This study proposes a three-dimensional resolved numerical framework for analysing complex shape polyhedron–fluid interaction. The weakly compressible smoothed particle hydrodynamics (SPH) is employed to describe the complex hydrodynamic behaviour, and the discrete element method (DEM) is applied to solve the motion of irregular polyhedral granular materials. An accurate contact collision algorithm is implemented in the coupling model to address the dynamic response of polyhedral particles. The high-fidelity modelling of the complex morphology of granular materials is realised. Hence, the coupling code is developed based on two high-performance open-source platforms, and an efficient two-way coupling scheme is designed to manage the fluid–particle interaction under a unified time framework. The reliability and the applicability of the SPH-DEM solver are demonstrated through extensive validations based on existing experimental and numerical data involving violent free-surface flow impacting multiple rigid bodies and cube deposition. Furthermore, a more complex case of wave-flow impact on rock piles is conducted, exhibiting great potential to employ this model in real-life engineering problems.

Introduction

It is critical to understand the interaction mechanism of fluid and granular materials that allows us to analyse better many crucial real-world engineering problems, such as solid–liquid flow in deep-sea mining pipelines (Xu et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022), the interaction of waves with coastal rocks and armour blocks (Mitsui et al., 2023), landslides form debris flow phenomena (Bu et al., 2022) and rock dumping process in water (Liu et al., 2020). Accurately predicting large-scale fluid–solid complex interactions remains challenging. Furthermore, the granular materials in real applications mostly show irregular shapes (Xu et al., 2022), posing a greater challenge for simulations. Although the experimental means can observe the motion state of particle groups, it is challenging to accurately monitor the force between particles and fluid due to the limitations of experimental equipment and the interference of unknown factors in the
The discrete element method (DEM) (Cundall ODLS, 1979), the most widely used for simulating granular material, provides insight into the discontinuous and heterogeneous properties of actual operation. Many researchers increasingly favour the numerical techniques developed gradually because they accurately capture flow characteristics and solid dynamics. granular materials (Zohdi, 2017). The granular materials simulated by the DEM are regarded as a separate DEM particle that carries information, such as shape, speed and location, and these particles will interact. The DEM tracks the motion of DEM particles according to Newton's law, a pure Lagrangian description (Liu and Xu, 2023). The motion prediction of the whole particle cluster is realised based on the track monitoring of each particle. DEM simulations can accurately measure the mechanical behaviour of complex shape particles at the particle scale (Govender et al., 2018) and have gradually become the most popular numerical method to simulate particle interaction problems. DEM simulations are computationally demanding, making industrial applications involving large-scale particle interaction calculations focus on spheroidal particle systems. However, these simplifications might exhibit unrealistic mechanical behaviour when predicting the dynamic properties of particle systems. Therefore, many researchers began to develop advanced DEM contact detection algorithms (Hawker et al., 2018), such as polymorphic particle characterisation techniques (Govender et al., 2014) or other ways, to enhance the capabilities of the DEM for simulating the dynamic behaviour of complex particle materials (Yang et al., 2023; Zurovec et al., 2019), thereby incorporating the fundamental factors needed for engineering applications that they are keen to observe. The typical modelling of non-spherical particles includes the multi-sphere model (Abou-Chakra et al., 2004), super quadrics (Cleary and Sawley, 2002) and convex polyhedrons (Govender et al., 2017).
Therein, polyhedron-shaped particles represent most granular materials well and can exhibit the realistic mechanical properties of actual particle systems, frequently applied to consider the influence of complex shape factors on granular mechanics with great success (Govender et al., 2015). A polyhedron is represented as a collection of planar faces, feature edges used in the detailed volume contact method and vertices. This modelling form is used to characterise particle morphology in this work.
The fluid domain primarily includes grid- and particle-based numerical methods. Significant achievements have been made in applying traditional grid-based computational fluid dynamics (CFD) methods in ocean engineering, where typical examples include the finite volume method (FVM) (Mao et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2022). Although the grid-based method is widely used in fluid–solid interaction problems, capturing large deformations, complex moving interfaces or free liquid surfaces is challenging. Even if re-meshing the complex fluid–solid interface can be used to improve the accuracy and stability (Khayyer et al., 2022), it will increase the difficulty of solving and negatively affect the computational efficiency (Ma et al., 2022). Furthermore, the geometric complexity of particle shapes in the real environment frequently leads to mesh distortion. In the latter method, meshless particle methods in the Lagrangian framework can well overcome the mesh-dependence problem posed by the traditional grid methods (Khayyer et al., 2019). Meshless CFD methods, such as Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) (Huang et al., 2023; Khayyer et al., 2023), Moving Particle Semi-Implicit (MPS) (Xu et al., 2023), Radial Basis Function (RBF) (Xiao et al., 2023), and Finite Pointset Method (FPM) (Saucedo-Zendejo and Resendiz-Flores, 2022), offer great potential for effectively addressing complex nonlinear hydrodynamic problems. SPH discretizes fluids using particles, making it well-suited for simulating free surface flows and large deformations. MPS combines finite point and particle approaches to handle complex boundaries and free surfaces. RBF provides accurate approximations of flow variables by utilizing radial basis functions, proving particularly effective for problems with irregular geometries and complex boundary conditions. FPM discretizes the physical domain using a finite number of nodes and establishes associations between nodes and function values. It offers versatility in handling problems with intricate geometries and moving boundaries. Specially, coupled SPH/MPS-DEM can solve free surface and large deformation problems and capture complex moving interfaces without additional numerical techniques (Harada et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2021). Moreover, the coupling of Lagrangian methods is straightforward in constructing the algorithm framework, making it highly compatible with the graphics processing unit (GPU) parallel technology for effectively addressing large-scale particle flow problems. This coupling form has gradually gained popularity among scholars (Luo et al., 2021) and is regarded as a promising candidate within the coupled CFD-DEM framework.
Currently, the SPH-DEM coupling model has been successfully applied in many research fields, such as the impact of waves on breakwater blocks (Sarfaraz and Pak, 2017, 2018; Zhai et al., 2023), landslide-generated waves (Bu et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021), submerged sphere interaction (Zou et al., 2022) or rapid flow scour (Tang et al., 2018). With the continuous development of coupling models, their accuracy, stability and applicability are constantly enhanced. Coupled SPH-DEM methods can be categorised into unresolved and resolved approaches according to the mode of modelling fluid–particle interaction. In the unresolved method, the force between the fluid and the particle primarily depends on the relative velocity and the local solid volume fraction, obtained from empirical formulas. Based on this coupling strategy, the SPH-DEM method has been applied to many research fields (Bu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2023). The resolved coupling method can obtain the fluid force on non-spherical DEM particles without relying on classical empirical formulas (Peng et al., 2021). DEM particles are regarded as the moving boundaries in the fluid domain. The hydrodynamic forces on DEM particles are calculated based on the particle surface integral of the fluid–solid interaction force. In order to accurately capture the intricate dynamics of fluid and its interaction with complex boundaries, the resolved SPH-DEM method necessitates a smaller spacing between SPH particles when compared to the unresolved SPH-DEM method. Generally, for more accurate SPH-DEM simulations, the DEM particle diameter should exceed eight times the fluid–particle spacing (Shen et al., 2022). The above indicates that the resolved coupling method is suitable for simulating the interaction of coarse particles with complex morphology and fluid, and supports us in obtaining the meso-scale perspective of fluid flow in granular media.
To the best of the authors’ knowledge, one primary challenge in simulating the fluid–solid interaction in multiple physical fields, especially involving irregular shapes, is the accurate monitoring of complex interface contact and the efficient solution of multi-body collision forces in rigid body dynamics. Canelas et al. (2016) adopted the distributed contact (DC) DEM in the coupled framework with SPH to address the multi-body contact collision problem and realised the numerical solution by directly considering the solid–solid and solid–fluid interactions. This approach was validated for debris flows correlating well with the experimental data (Canelas et al., 2017). Peng et al. (2021) enhanced the coupling SPH-DEM algorithm by proposing a simple unified hybrid contact model to achieve satisfactory results in simulating debris flows. Asai et al. (2021) used the energy-tracking impulse method to solve the friction contact problem more accurately and effectively, achieving better energy conservation in an SPH-DEM coupling framework. Although some progress has been made in modelling the shape of non-spherical particles, challenges to modelling complex shapes in nature remain. Particle–particle and particle–fluid interactions must be carefully processed, and the computational pressure caused by large-scale engineering applications must be well borne.
This study provides an effective coupling framework that combines the advantages of SPH in processing complex interface captures with large deformation and the ability of DEM to monitor the mechanical behaviour of rigid particles to manage the fine-scale interaction of the fluid–solid system. Several salient features of this study are i) establishing a two-way coupling SPH-DEM algorithm and employing the polyhedron model to model the irregular particle system. ii) The study further implants a superior contact collision detection method into the fluid–solid coupling frame, guaranteeing shape fidelity and significantly improving the accuracy of polyhedral collision simulation. iii) Coupled code is developed based on two high-performance open-source platforms: DualSPHysics and Blaze-DEM. DualSPHysics specialises in non-linear free-surface flow and violent interaction problems, while Blaze-DEM can manage the complex contact of irregularly shaped granular materials. Both solvers support GPU parallelism, enhancing the model's ability to address large-scale three-dimensional (3D) problems. Furthermore, the operability of the open-source platform provides convenience for the subsequent model optimisation.
This study is presented in six sections. Section 2 displays the primary formulation included in the SPH-based DualSPHysics model. Section 3 introduces the solution of polyhedron contact in the Blaze-DEM model, and Section 4 presents the implementation details of the coupling procedure. Section 5 verifies the accuracy of the model and performs an application extension. Section 6 exhibits the conclusion of this study.

    • Resolved DEM-CFD coupling for wave-armour blocks interactions

      2025, Ocean Engineering
      The present work aims to tackle breakwater stability challenges through an innovative numerical deterministic method using a resolved DEM-CFD (Discrete Element Method—Computational Fluid Dynamics) strategy, which simulates the individual motions of armour units within a fluid solver. To achieve this, a coupling between a DEM code and a CFD code is implemented and validated. The fluids (air and water) are solved using a Eulerian–Eulerian CFD solver, and the contacts between blocks are solved using a DEM code. The solids are defined within the fluid solver using a discrete forcing approach and are therefore fully resolved. In this way, the fluid solver enables the prediction of object motions with complex shapes such as tetrapods. To couple the codes, forces exerted on the solids are calculated in the fluid solver and sent to the DEM solver. Then, contact and gravity forces are computed and added to the fluid forces. The DEM solver then computes the new positions and velocities of the bodies, which are retrieved by the fluid solver. An experimental study is performed on a fixed and instrumented idealized breakwater to evaluate the wave forces acting on a coastal structure. The experiments are then numerically reproduced to validate the numerical model. Simulations of the impact of solitary waves on a row of mobile isolated tetrapods laid on a horizontal berm are then performed using the DEM-CFD coupling. The importance of initial placement and friction parameters is investigated to show the sensitivity to these parameters.

    • A review of flow-induced vibration in wind and oceanic flow: Mechanisms, applications, optimizations, and challenges

      2025, Ocean Engineering
      This paper provides a comprehensive review of the latest advancements in flow-induced vibration (FIV) and its two primary applications: vibration suppression and energy harvesting. The focus is on the interrelationship between the underlying mechanisms and these applications. First, key factors such as mass ratio, damping ratio, Reynolds number, and stiffness are examined for their influences on vibration response, with attention to recent advancements in the understanding of vortex-induced vibration (VIV), galloping, buffeting, and flutter. Second, the paper reviews modeling methods for FIV, with particular emphasis on the non-conformal mesh method and the integration of machine learning. The latest optimization strategies for both vibration suppression and energy harvesting are then explored, alongside the challenges associated with each. FIV energy harvesters are categorized based on the working fluid—wind (FIVEHW) and oceanic flow (FIVEHO)—highlighting the effects of flow density (mass ratio) and the unpredictability of flow direction and velocity on the selection of energy transducers, energy evaluation criteria and output scales. Optimization methods for vibration inhibition and energy harvesting are divided into two main categories: (1) direct methods, which directly modify the dynamic behavior governed by the FIV motion equation through the alteration of structural parameters (e.g., stiffness and damping), and the introduction of nonlinearity and multi-stabilities; (2) indirect methods, which regulate flow separation and vortex shedding to control vibration amplitude via geometric modifications (e.g., cross-sectional shapes, surface attachments, and surface roughness), jetting and blowing techniques, and the optimization of multiple oscillators for wake stabilization or wake interference. The paper concludes with a summary and discussion of the current state of the field, identifying existing capabilities and limitations and recommending areas for future research to address remaining gaps.

    • Study on the ice-water interaction problem based on MPS-NDEM coupling model

      2025, Engineering Analysis with Boundary Elements
      Ice-water coupling is a unique fluid-solid interaction problem characterized by collisions and hydrodynamic interaction between multiple bodies, accompanied by significant changes in the free surface. This paper presents a novel numerical model that achieves two-way coupling between the moving particle semi-implicit (MPS) method and the non-smooth discrete element method (NDEM) to simulate ice-water interactions. The MPS method is employed to model fluid motion, while the NDEM is applied to simulate the motion and collision of ice floes. The improved MPS method presented in this study, based on our previous research, addresses several issues found in the traditional MPS method. To improve the computational efficiency, the domain decomposition method is implemented to parallel the fluid solver and the AMG preconditioned GMRES iterative solver is selected as the optimal solution scheme for the improved MPS method. The accuracy and feasibility of the present coupling model are validated through benchmark cases, such as dam break, wave motion, ice motion in regular waves and debris dam break flow. Furthermore, the simulation of the ship moving in the broken ice field is successfully conducted with the coupling model and the influence of ice-water coupling load on predicting broken ice resistance is also investigated.

    • SPH simulations of complex 3D fluid–solid interactions with an improved single-layer particle boundary technique preventing boundary penetration

      2024, Ocean Engineering
      The smoothed particle hydrodynamics (SPH) method has been widely used to simulate fluid–solid interaction (FSI) problems. The solid boundary treatment under complex geometric configurations has always been a hot topic in the SPH numerical simulation. An improved single-layer particle technique combined with an effective anti-penetration boundary improvement algorithm is proposed in this paper, which allows for multi-resolution single-layer particle distribution and effectively prevents particle penetration under complex geometric configurations. Four engineering problems are simulated with the improved technique: dam-break flows with a rectangular prism, water entry of a revolution body, oscillations of a floating body, and gearbox operation.
