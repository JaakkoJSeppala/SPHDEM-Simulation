\abstract{%
This thesis investigates the applicability of SPH–DEM (Smoothed Particle 
Hydrodynamics–Discrete Element Method) modeling for simulating granular dampers
 in mitigating sloshing in ship ballast tanks. The aim was to assess how
  effectively the SPH–DEM approach captures the dynamic interaction between
   liquid sloshing and granular dampers, and its potential to reduce 
   sloshing-induced structural loads.
The study is based on a literature review, mathematical modeling, and numerical
 simulations, without relying on industrial measurement data. SPH was used to
  model the fluid dynamics, while DEM was employed to simulate the behavior of
   granular materials. The simulations focused on the free-surface dynamics of
    the fluid and the energy dissipation capacity of granular dampers.
The results demonstrate that SPH–DEM modeling can effectively represent the 
interaction between sloshing and granular dampers, though the accuracy of the 
method is significantly influenced by particle resolution and interaction 
models. The findings suggest that the SPH–DEM approach holds promise for 
supporting the design of granular dampers, although further research is needed
 for large-scale industrial applications.

\section{Introduction}
Ship ballast tanks are essential for maintaining vessel stability and 
operability. When partially filled, these tanks are prone to liquid sloshing, 
which can generate significant dynamic loads on the tank structure~\parencite{krata2013}. 
Such loads may compromise structural integrity, increase maintenance costs,
 and in extreme cases endanger human safety~\parencite{lyu2019}. Consequently,
  effective mitigation of sloshing-induced forces is a critical concern in 
  naval architecture and offshore engineering.

Various passive and active techniques have been developed to reduce sloshing 
effects. Baffles, such as horizontal obstructions or screens, can suppress
 higher-order sloshing modes~\parencite{konar2023deep}. Tuned mass dampers (TMDs)
  provide additional vibration control by absorbing energy at specific 
  frequencies~\parencite{gao2020frequency}, while tuned liquid dampers (TLDs)
   exploit liquid sloshing to achieve similar damping effects~\parencite{dominizio2024}.

Granular dampers offer a simple, robust, and power-free solution. They dissipate
 energy through inelastic collisions and friction among enclosed particles, 
 providing broadband vibration attenuation without active control or extensive
  maintenance~\parencite{lu2018particle}. Despite promising experimental
   results, numerical modeling of granular dampers in sloshing environments 
   remains challenging, as traditional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) often 
   struggles to capture both free-surface flows and discrete particle
    interactions~\parencite{ferziger2020computational}.

The coupled Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) and Discrete Element Method (DEM)
 framework offers a promising approach. SPH handles free-surface fluid dynamics,
  while DEM explicitly models particle–particle and particle–wall interactions~\parencite{monaghan1994sph}. 
  Recent SPH–DEM studies demonstrate improved damping performance in liquid–particle
   dampers compared to dry particle dampers~\parencite{gnanasambandham2018},
    yet its potential in ship ballast tanks has not been systematically evaluated.

The central research question of this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
\textit{How effective is the SPH–DEM approach in capturing the dynamic interaction 
between liquid sloshing and granular dampers, and to what extent can it predict
 the reduction of sloshing-induced structural loads?}
\end{quote}

This study addresses the question through numerical simulations using a coupled 
SPH–DEM framework. The focus is on modeling fluid motion and granular energy
 dissipation, while experimental validation and industrial-scale optimization
  are left for future research.

The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows: Chapter~2 reviews the
 theoretical background of SPH, DEM, and sloshing dynamics. Chapter~3 presents
  the numerical methodology and implementation details. Chapter~4 describes the
   simulation results, with analysis and discussion in Chapter~5. Finally,
    Chapter~6 summarizes the main conclusions and outlines directions for
     future work.

\section{Granular Dampers}
Granular dampers (also called particle dampers) are passive devices consisting of
an enclosure filled with loose particles attached to a vibrating structure
\textcite{prasad2024}. Energy dissipation occurs through repeated inelastic
collisions and frictional contacts both between the particles and with the
enclosure walls. Consequently, the damping performance is highly sensitive to
the particle properties---such as size, shape, and stiffness---and to the
geometry and filling ratio of the container \textcite{prasad2024}.

Because these collision and frictional interactions are inherently nonlinear and
complex, analytical models are impractical for realistic systems. Therefore,
most studies employ numerical approaches such as the Discrete Element Method
(DEM) \textcite{zhu2025}. In cases where fluid--particle interactions must also
be considered, DEM can be coupled with Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) to
capture two-way coupling between particles and the surrounding fluid
\textcite{liu2022}.

Several practical benefits of granular dampers have been reported in the literature.
First, they can provide broadband vibration attenuation by reducing vibration amplitudes across a wide range of frequencies rather than only at a single resonance~\parencite{schonle2022}.
Second, their mechanically simple design requires no active control and very little maintenance~\parencite{heckel2012}.
Granular dampers can be tailored to different geometries and scales, with applications ranging from marine to aerospace structures~\parencite{prasad2024}.
Finally, granular dampers are highly versatile: they can be adapted to different geometries and scales, with demonstrated or proposed applications in marine structures and aerospace systems~\parencite{avdic2024}.


\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH)}

Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is a meshless, Lagrangian method where the fluid domain is discretized into moving particles carrying mass, momentum, and energy. Field quantities are computed using kernel interpolation functions based on the neighboring particles' positions. SPH is particularly suitable for problems with large free-surface deformations and fragmentation, such as sloshing or multiphase flows. 

A comprehensive review of the development and applications of SPH can be found in \textcite{Monaghan1992} and more recently in \textcite{Liu2023}. Despite its flexibility, SPH suffers from issues such as pressure oscillations, kernel inconsistency, and numerical instability under high-density ratios. These challenges are especially critical in coupled fluid–structure or fluid–solid systems.

\subsection{Discrete Element Method (DEM)}

The Discrete Element Method (DEM) models the behavior of granular materials by tracking the motion and interactions of individual particles. Each particle obeys Newton's equations of motion, while contact forces (normal and tangential) are computed using predefined collision and friction models. 

DEM is widely used in the study of granular damping, powders, and particulate flows. As reviewed by \textcite{Cundall1979} and \textcite{Zhu2007}, DEM accurately resolves particle dynamics but becomes computationally expensive for systems with millions of particles, requiring coupling strategies when interacting with fluids.

\subsection{SPH–DEM Coupling Principles}

To model the interaction between a fluid and discrete particles—such as in granular dampers or sediment transport—SPH and DEM can be coupled in two primary ways: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Resolved coupling:} fluid forces are resolved at the particle scale, directly computing hydrodynamic stresses on individual particles.
  \item \textbf{Unresolved coupling:} the fluid phase is averaged over control volumes, and drag and buoyancy forces are estimated based on local porosity and velocity gradients.
\end{itemize}

A recent overview of coupling strategies is presented in \textcite{Sun2023} and \textcite{Tian2025}. The coupling requires consistent interpolation of fluid variables onto DEM particles and a force-feedback mechanism ensuring momentum conservation between the phases. Typically, the drag force is modeled according to empirical correlations, such as the Ergun or Wen–Yu equations, adjusted for local porosity.

\subsection{Limitations and Numerical Challenges}

Despite its effectiveness, SPH–DEM coupling involves several numerical challenges:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Numerical stability:} Large density or viscosity contrasts can induce instabilities in the pressure field.
  \item \textbf{Particle shape:} Non-spherical particles require extended models, such as superquadric DEM formulations \parencite{Zhou2023}.
  \item \textbf{Parameter sensitivity:} Drag coefficients, contact stiffness, and smoothing lengths significantly affect results \parencite{Tran2024}.
  \item \textbf{Computational cost:} Fully resolved SPH–DEM simulations are expensive for large domains; unresolved coupling offers efficiency at the cost of reduced accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Applications to Sloshing and Granular Damping}

Granular dampers in ballast tanks utilize solid particles to dissipate the kinetic energy of liquid sloshing. The fluid–particle interaction modifies wave dynamics, reduces peak pressures, and enhances energy dissipation through inelastic collisions.

Several studies have applied SPH–DEM methods to sloshing and damping phenomena. For instance, \textcite{Liu2019} validated SPH simulations of sloshing tanks with experimental data, while \textcite{Lu2024} analyzed the effect of submerged particles on the attenuation of free-surface oscillations. These works demonstrate that coupled SPH–DEM models can accurately capture nonlinear interactions between fluids and granular media.

This theoretical chapter provides an overview of the SPH and DEM methods, their coupling strategies, and the main numerical challenges associated with hybrid simulations. The combination of SPH and DEM enables detailed modeling of fluid–particle systems, though it requires careful parameter calibration and validation. The theoretical foundations discussed here form the basis for the numerical modeling and validation presented in the following chapters.

\section{Overview}
Due to the unavailability of industrial measurement data, the empirical investigation in this study is based on numerical simulations. 
The objective is to assess the performance of granular dampers in mitigating sloshing by exploring the dynamic behavior of the coupled fluid–particle system under a range of theoretically justified conditions. 
Simulations were designed to provide insights into parameter dependencies and to validate the numerical models qualitatively and quantitatively against benchmark cases and literature.

\section{Simulation Setup}
This setup enables systematic testing of how granular damping influences sloshing-induced loads, thereby addressing the main research objective.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SPH–DEM Coupling:} The fluid is modeled using Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH), while the granular material is represented by the Discrete Element Method (DEM). 
    Bidirectional coupling accounts for drag, pressure, and buoyancy forces between fluid and particles.
    \item \textbf{Domain and Geometry:} A rectangular tank with adjustable fill level is used to represent a generic ballast tank. Granular dampers are modeled as enclosed particles of defined size and density.
    \item \textbf{Boundary Conditions:} Solid walls are treated as rigid, no-slip boundaries for the fluid, with collision handling for DEM particles.
    \item \textbf{Initial Conditions:} Fluid and particles are initially at rest. Perturbations are introduced via prescribed tank motion to initiate sloshing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Parameter Selection and Sensitivity Analysis}
This section details the selection of numerical and physical parameters used in the SPH–DEM simulations. Conducting a sensitivity analysis ensures that the observed damping performance is not an artifact of specific parameter choices but reflects the underlying physical behavior of the coupled liquid–particle system.

In the absence of industrial data, parameters were chosen based on literature values and physical reasoning. 
A systematic sensitivity study was conducted to examine the influence of each parameter on damping performance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Particle size, shape, and number.
    \item Particle stiffness, restitution coefficient, and friction coefficient.
    \item Fluid density and viscosity.
    \item Tank fill level and damper placement.
\end{itemize}
For each simulation, energy dissipation, root-mean-square (RMS) amplitudes, and force fluctuations were computed to quantify damping effectiveness.
